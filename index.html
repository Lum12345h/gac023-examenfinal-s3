1. Cell Theory

Biology is the study of life. Modern biology rests on several pillars, one of which is Cell Theory.

Core Principles

  • All organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  • Cells arise only from pre-existing cells.

History & Key Figures

Robert Hooke (1635–1703): One of the first to use a microscope. He examined cork and coined the term "cells" because they looked like monk's rooms.
  • Matthias Schleiden (1838): Concluded all plant tissues are made of cells.
  • Theodore Schwann (1839): Concluded all animal tissues are made of cells.
  • Rudolf Virchow (1858): Added the conclusion that all cells come from pre-existing cells.

Implication: There is an unbroken "chain-of-existence" from your cells today back to the earliest cells 3.5 billion years ago.

2. Photosynthesis

The process by which plants use light energy and chlorophyll to transform carbon dioxide and water into food (glucose) and oxygen.

The Chemical Reaction

Water + Carbon Dioxide → Glucose + Oxygen
6H₂O + 6CO₂ → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂

Key Components

  • Chlorophyll: The green pigment required to trap light energy.
  • Glucose: High-energy food. Plants convert this into starch for storage or cellulose for building structure (wood).

Testing for Photosynthesis

Experiments in the manual confirm that both Light and Carbon Dioxide are required.

  • Starch Test: Boil leaf in alcohol (to remove green color), add iodine. Blue-black color = Starch present.
  • De-starching: Keep plant in dark for 48 hours to use up stored starch before testing.

3. Classification of Living Things

Scientists classify life to communicate universally. The system groups similar organisms and excludes different ones.

The 5 Kingdoms

  1. Animalia: Multicellular, eukaryotic, heterotrophic (eats food), mobile.
  2. Plantae: Multicellular, eukaryotic, autotrophic (photosynthesis).
  3. Fungi: Eukaryotic, heterotrophic (absorbs nutrients), cell walls of chitin (e.g., mushrooms, yeast).
  4. Protista: Eukaryotic, mostly unicellular (e.g., amoeba, algae).
  5. Prokaryotae (Monera): Unicellular, no nucleus (e.g., bacteria).

Linnaean Hierarchy

Devised by Carl von Linné (Linnaeus). From broad to specific:

Kingdom → Phylum → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species
Memory Aid: Kings Play Chess On Fine Grain Sand

Binomial Nomenclature

Naming species using Latin. Format: Genus species (e.g., Homo sapiens). The genus is capitalized; species is lowercase.

4. Microbial Life

Prokaryotes

Organisms without a nucleus (Bacteria and Archaea). They are microscopic (1-10 µm) and outnumber all other living things.

  • Impact: Can cause disease (Black Death, Cholera) but are also essential for digestion, skin health, and nutrient cycling.
  • Bioremediation: Using bacteria to clean oil spills or hazardous waste.

Shapes of Bacteria

  • Cocci: Spherical (e.g., Streptococci).
  • Spirilla: Spiral-shaped (e.g., Syphilis bacteria).

Viruses

Non-living parasites. They consist only of a protein coat (capsid) and a nucleic acid core (DNA or RNA). They cannot reproduce on their own; they must hijack a host cell.

5. Ecosystems

Ecology is the study of interactions between organisms and their environment.

Structure

  • Biotic Factors: Living components (plants, animals).
  • Abiotic Factors: Non-living components (temperature, water, rocks, energy).
  • Hierarchy: Organism → Population → Community → Ecosystem.

Energy Flow (Trophic Levels)

Energy enters from the Sun and moves through levels. Only about 10% of energy transfers from one level to the next.

  1. Producers (Autotrophs): Plants, Algae.
  2. Primary Consumers (Herbivores): Eat plants.
  3. Secondary Consumers (Carnivores): Eat herbivores.
  4. Tertiary Consumers: Top predators.

Food Web: A complex network of interconnected food chains.

6. The Biosphere

The Biosphere is the giant, interconnected machine of Earth. It includes all ecosystems, the Earth's crust (Lithosphere), waters (Hydrosphere), and air (Atmosphere).

Cycling

Earth is a closed system for matter. Elements like Carbon, Nitrogen, and Water are constantly recycled through the biosphere via biological and geological processes.

7. Aquatic & Terrestrial Ecosystems

Aquatic Ecosystems

Life originated in the sea. Water acts as a stable environment due to high heat capacity.

  • Plankton: Drifting organisms.
    • Phytoplankton: Producers (e.g., diatoms, green algae).
    • Zooplankton: Consumers (e.g., krill, microscopic larvae).
  • Vulnerability: Waterways integrate pollution from the entire land watershed, making them sensitive to sewage and runoff.

Terrestrial Ecosystems (Biomes)

Land-based ecosystems defined primarily by temperature and rainfall.

The 9 Major Biomes: Tropical Rainforest, Savannah, Desert (Hot/Cold), Polar Ice, Chaparral, Temperate Grasslands, Temperate Deciduous Forest, Taiga (Coniferous Forest), Tundra.

8. Earth Resources: Rocks & Soils

Structure of Earth

  • Crust: Thin outer layer (25-40km thick).
  • Mantle: Dense rock, partly liquid (magma).
  • Core: Liquid outer, solid inner (Iron/Sulfur).

Rock Types

  • Igneous: Cooled magma/lava. Granite (cooled slowly underground), Basalt (cooled quickly on surface).
  • Sedimentary: Compressed sediments. Sandstone, Shale, Limestone.
  • Metamorphic: Changed by heat/pressure. Limestone becomes Marble.

Soils & Erosion

  • Composition: Weathered minerals (Sand, Silt, Clay) + Humus (organic matter).
  • pH: Measures acidity. Ideal for plants is 5.5–7.5.
  • Erosion: Removal of soil by wind or water.
    • Water Erosion: Causes rills and gullies. Prevented by terraces/tree cover.
    • Wind Erosion: Common in arid areas (e.g., loess soil).
